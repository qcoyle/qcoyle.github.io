Salary

Why this negotiation matters

  • An outsized influence on your compensation based on the amount of time (typically <5 min to do it). Your salary negotiations are probably the most important financial decisions you make - they have even more leverage than a house price negotiation
  • It compounds with your whole career! Over 10 years, the value of $5,000 a year extra is close to $100k gross
  • Compensation includes money, time, opportunities to do fulfilling tasks, and other perks that make a “meaningful difference in your day to day quality of life”[a]
  • It’s life. Negotiation is an “all day every day thing” in much of the business world

Fully loaded costs

  • Typically the fully loaded cost of an employee (healthcare, payroll taxes, HR overhead, other benefits) is ~150-200% of their salary. Companies view the costs of employees in these terms
  • “Virtually any amount of money available to you personally is mouse droppings to your prospective employer” - Patrick McKenzie
  • As such, if you’re an engineer and see an extra $5,000 as a lot - companies don’t really. That’s only 3% above their fully-loaded cost of you, and a tiny fraction of a percent of their overall hiring budget

On moral concerns

  • Patrick McKenzie brings up a few points to consider:
  • The HR manager (or your potential future manager, if talking to them) is not spending their own money to hire you. They are spending their budget. Performance incentives are typically not too closely tied to shaving money off this budget
  • For many managers, power + status comes from asking for more budget every year. So if you turn out to be expensive (and worth it) this is great for the manager because they will then get more budget next quarter
  • You don’t owe the manager for giving you the job opportunity. If the deal makes economic sense, it will happen. If it doesn’t, “firm handshakes will be exchanged, non-specific promises will be uttered, and everyone will forget about this discussion in a matter of hours”
  • The manager is “far, far less invested in the negotiation than you are.” They’re working through a list of a dozen people, have other project deadlines to meet, and personal lives that are (and should be) more important to them than this negotiation

When to negotiate

  • Do not start negotiating until there is a Yes-if agreement. Any discussion prior to a Yes-If agreement is premature. “The ideal resolution to a job interview is for both sides to be optimistic about the arrangement”
  • Only negotiate after you have an agreement in principle from someone with hiring authority that, after mutually acceptable compensation can be agreed upon you will be hired
  • This means the company will spend a lot of time + effort to getting to the point of agreeing in principle to a good fit. The company may spend thousands of dollars just talking to you and in conversations about you. As such they want to reach an agreement and not lose out on that investment
  • This also means that the absolute worst outcome of negotiating is that you get the value in the offer. If they’ve spent so many resources on you, they’re not going to rescind the offer because you negotiated. “Negotiating never makes (worthwhile) offers worse”
  • Note: you don’t start negotiating from a No-But (“No-But we might hire you anyways if you are cheap enough”). You don’t want to work for a No-But and good employers don’t want to hire candidates who are a No-But 

Where to negotiate

  • Over email is likely to your advantage over in realtime
  • This gives max time to prepare responses, especially for engineers as you might be talking to an experienced negotiator

Never give a number first

  • Patrick McKenzie says you aren’t doing anything immoral or unprofessional. You are talking to business people whose job it is is to make decisions when they don’t have all the information they would like to
  • Probably all the employees they really value were “not a doormat at negotiations.” When managers think of winners, they think of good business people like them and you might also continue to get compared to those types of people. He says you will get judged accordingly if you give a number like that
  • Good things to say instead of a number:
  • “I’m more concerned at the moment with talking to you about discovering whether we’re a mutual fit.  If we’re a great fit, then I can be flexible on the numbers with you and you can be flexible on the numbers with me.  If we’re not a great fit, then the numbers are ultimately irrelevant, because your company only hires A players and I only work at roles I would be an A player at”
  • “I know you’re concerned about $FILL_IN_THE_BLANK.  In addition to my previous successes doing it, I have some great ideas for what I’d do about that if I was working at your company.  Would you like to drill into those or is there another job area you’re more concerned about to start with?”
  • If you have to give a number: “Well, you know, salary is only one component of the total compensation package.  In terms of total compensation, we’re probably looking at something like $FILL_IN_NUMBER_HERE.”  (Suggested calculation: take the package value from your last company and add 5~10%.  If you don’t know how to calculate the value of your compensation package, learn that, but as a rough guesstimate salary + 30 ~ 50% for full-time employees in professional roles and the multiplier tends to scale up as your base salary scales up.)”

A better strategy for job searching

  • Instead of seeing an add on LInkedin, sending in a resume, getting an interview, getting asked for salary requirements, getting offered your salary requirement + 5%, and negotiating a little bit, Patrick McKenzie recommends the following process:
  1. Establish a reputation in your field as someone who delivers measurable results by innovating → improving revenue or reducing costs
  2. Have a hiring manager talk to you specifically about an opening they want you to specifically fulfill
  3. Talk informally and possibly formally with team members. Come to the conclusion that it would be a great thing if both sides came to a mutually fulfilling offer
  4. Let them take a stab on what a mutually fulfilling offer would be
  5. Suggest ways that the company could improve the offer so that “the path is cleared for you to” do what you are known to do in improving their revenue/reducing costs
  6. Optional: give the person hiring you a resume to send to HR for their records. Don’t expect it to be read

Other advice

  • From Patrick McKenzie:
  • “Interesting” is a wonderful word. (i.e. saying $XXX is “interesting” after they offer you something). It’s positive and non-committal at the same time. Always say a number is “interesting” and not “good” or “wonderful”
  • Saying you’ll have to talk this over with your significant other (or other external authority) is a good tactic. Plus in the US your would-be employer is prohibited from prying about your marital status. Also saying “That sounds reasonable, but I’ll have to run it by the family” is a good way to delay
  • Note: companies also often use their own central authorities as good ways to delay. For instance, talking it over with the CEO
  • “I need this job because…”  You never need a job.  Being needy means that the party who is not needy has automatic leverage over you. Instead, aim for “I’m enthusiastic about the opportunity of working with you, assuming we can come to mutually satisfactory terms.”
  • Use their own words (from job postings, interviews) and repeat them back to people in saying what you can do
  • Take notes on a notebook at job interviews
  • When talking to current employees, a lot of employees will, out of company loyalty, attempt to sell you on taking the job with the company by trading you very useful information
  • The more you know, the more options you have during a negotiation
  • If the person you’re talking to cannot deviate but say that they wish they can, express sympathy and try to negotiate other benefits. This takes advantage of their unspent “You wanted to do something nice for me”

Robotics

  • More efficiency, fewer people driving to factories, new innovative products → reduced emissions
  • Note that this is referring to automation of physical work. It is not referring to robotic processing automation (RPA), which automates work tasks using algorithms
  • Robotics can be broken down into:
  • Physical structure (includes end effectors),
  • Motor(s) to move physical structure (including actuators)
  • Power source
  • Sensors to collect data about surroundings
  • Computer to act as the brains
  • 2020 robotics market was $50B, expected to become $60B in 2025
  • The market and growth in China is huge too, driven in part by its aging population. Labor salaries there have more than doubled since 2010
  • Cobots are expected to be the next wave of innovation
  • In addition to being safer, human-machine groups are more efficient than entire teams consisting of one or the other according to MIT (not sure what industry this was in)
  • Cobots also reduce idle time

Space

  • A long term idea is doing most of our heavy industry in space; scaling up to that. This would reduce emissions on earth while supporting economies of people in space
  • 2020 and 2021 have been banner years for space. There are many risks, but it could be the start of a continued positive feedback loop in decreasing launch costs. Anything being launched to space now for commercial reasons is helping reduce these launch costs by boosting demand. This could eventually lead to some helpful things for the environment
  • Some products are said to be much more economical to manufacture in space. Space could allow for a moore’s law for those products, including:
  • Semiconductors. Some say quality could increase by 10,000% due to lack of dust in space. Cheaper fabs as well
  • Fiber optic cables. Fewer interruptive crystals
  • Pharma
  • Printing Human organs: cells don’t collapse under their own weight. Anticipated it might be 5-10 years out. No scaffolding needed to print them
  • Patients receive organs from their own stem cells - no rejection as a result
  • Print meat in space. Can make full steaks. This can feed astronauts on long missions
  • Creating more efficient solar panels in space: Gallium Arsenide is a better conductor of solar energy than silicon. 40% efficiency, vs. 15% in Silicon.
  • Gallium Arsenide manufacturing releases toxic chemicals on earth, including Arsenic
  • Vacuum of space allows product to grow and layers one atom high to be made. Layers are then stacked on each other in the final product
  • Estimated increase solar panel efficiency by 60%
  • On a large enough scale, it could fulfill all solar energy demands on earth
  • Make microchips in space
  • It’s a very water intensive process on earth
  • One 12in integrated circuit requires about 2200 gallons of water for cleaning and cooling. Water was part of the issue behind 2020 semiconductor shortage
  • Fewer environmental hazards
  • Make better metal alloys in space, as they can mix more easily in microgravity
  • Magnesium alloys for medical implants. They’re half the weight of titanium alloys and are similar to density/strength of human bones. They also don’t biodegrade in the body
  • Easier cooling
  • Impurities don’t settle at the bottom of metals
  • No oxidizing film forms on top
  • Many earth-focused applications for communication and imagery as well:
  • Satellite internet - 42 million americans (and 40% of the world) still don’t have access to fixed cellular or broadband
  • Starlink + other telecom satellites
  • Starlink has 1,385 satellites as of March 2021. Planning for 4,425 of them by 2024. Full constellation would be 12,000 satellites. SpaceX has talked about wanting to put up a total of 42,000 satellites eventually
  • In very low earth orbit, which is why they need so many of them
  • Lower orbit allows for less latency, which is why they do it
  • Estimated 70 million households around the world that have the demand for broadband and the capacity to pay - but don’t yet have it
  • Currently not yet enterprise grade, which is why it’s going towards consumers first. Could be done for businesses after
  • Lots of concerns:
  • All current satellite companies are subsidized by the government $ or the bankruptcy court. Iridium and Orbcomm went bankrupt in the 1990’s
  • All capital expenditure is needed upfront before customers
  • Distortion of telescope imagery
  • Interference of radio astronomy
  • Congestion concerns in space → space debris, although SpaceX says they will deorbit satellites. However if communication is lost or if propulsion fails, this doesn’t work. Could lead to a chain reaction of space debris
  • Imaging for crops, construction, autonomous vehicles, weather, etc. Can also help with better tracking of emissions
  • There has been an order of magnitude drop in launch costs in the last 10 years
  • Space shuttle 2000: $54k/kg
  • SpaceX 2021: $3k/kg and falling. Musk says starship will eventually hit $10/kg
  • If you believe him…a big if…that’s over 5 million % drop! A 54,000x decrease
  • Lots more infrastructure in Space now than there was 10 years ago
  • Companies
  • Varda is building space stations for manufacturing in space. Very young company with lots of backing. Aiming for mission in 1Q 2023. Payloads that sit atop SpaceX rockets. Microgravity as a service. Re-entry over land - which is part of their secret sauce
  • Astra: launch provider
  • Rocket Lab: launch provider
  • Made in Space. Doing 3D printed objects in space and trying to make fiber optic cable too. Machine shop in space business model
  • Starlink (SpaceX), Astranis, Amazon (said its investing over $10 billion in its satellite internet business), OneWeb (UK), Telesat are getting into internet from space
  • More than 20 rocket startups have now failed and at least 80 others are trying to get off the ground
  • Only two private companies are flying regular missions to space: SpaceX and Rocket Lab
  • Pro’s
  • There’s a ton of investment going into it. Billionaires investing. Lots of venture capital money as well. More government subsidies. Especially in the Starlink-like projects (FCC awarded SpaceX $900 million to bring the internet to rural places, Biden pledging more with infrastructure plan). This has got to be good for the industry
  • Possibility of manufacturing things that you can’t manufacture on earth. New value
  • Historically, finding new ways to make things has been the driver of economic growth. This would continue that
  • Mining industry is $1.7 Trillion. This could be done in space on asteroids
  • Taking materials mined in space to be used in space - primarily for fuel and water
  • Everyone is looking to set up shop on the moon to mine water

  • Space tourism as a luxury good

Carbon capture

  • CCUS: Carbon capture utilization and storage
  • General principles: requires power, water, and filters
  • Can be direct air capture, pre-combustion or post combustion
  • Direct air capture: Pull in atmospheric air and extract the CO2 using filters/chemicals. Compress the CO2 into liquid
  • Pre-combustion: Burn off the hydrogen, CO2 is compressed and stored
  • Post-combustion: Filtration of burn exhaust. Filters include basalt, ultra-porous crystals, ammonia, limestone, bacteria, or algae. Procuring these filter materials can cause CO2 emissions. Then compress the CO2. Can be mounted to existing power facilities
  • Why is it so hard to convert carbon back into fuel that can be used again?
  • It sounds like it’s a lot more expensive than current ways we extract fossil fuels
  • “The amount of energy required to capture and restore the carbon would likely (depending on what they are using to ‘capture’/solidify in) be similar in energy to the energy produced by freeing (burning) the carbon to begin with. If breaking a carbon off a chain produces 5 units of power, it would take 5 units of power to re-connect it to the chain. This power plant would produce zero power” (Reddit)
  • Support:
  • 90% of industrial emissions can be captured
  • Especially by filtering out CO2 from flue gases at plants using special solvents
  • IPCC has labeled it a necessity to keep it under control
  • Looks poised to become a main weapon in combating climate change, in addition to better energy efficiency and using renewables
  • Only 35 carbon capture facilities operating worldwide
  • Captured carbon can be quite easily used in concrete production. This also stores it away. Also concrete is responsible for 8% of greenhouse emissions, so this helps with that
  • 2018: US introduced a law that rewards companies for each metric ton of CO2 they capture and store. California also has its own laws
  • Big oil companies (i.e. Shell, Chevron, Total) are getting into it in Scandinavia
  • Criticisms:
  • Only two large scale coal plants in the world, and they had to be retrofitted
  • Hard to be profitable
  • Seems hard to establish a dominant role in the industry
  • Burying underground can be risky, leading to leak
  • It takes energy to convert captured carbon back into a usable energy source
  • Concerns about increased seismic activity underground if you bury it
  • A lot of current utilization for captured carbon is by the oil and gas industry to pump out more oil. However some companies like Climeworks are using it for carbonating drinks
  • Questions:
  • Why do experts think costs of carbon capture can be reduced by a factor of 3 or higher?
  • I don’t think burying it is a sustainable long term solution - are there studies to back this up?

Batteries

  • Prices went down by 10x from 2010 to 2020
  • Size of EV battery market in 2021: $23 Billion
  • Projected size of EV battery market in 2025: $70 Billion
  • Technologies
  • Lithium Ion is what’s currently used
  • Lithium Metal: Expected to match Li-Ion in 2028 in price by 2030. Increased energy density
  • Cuberg (from Standord) is pursuing a technology which they think can be half the cost. Uses liquid state lithium metal. They were acquired by Northvolt (HQ in Stockholm), which supplies BMW and VW. Currently in the aviation industry
  • QuantumScape: solid state lithium metal. Backed by Bill Gates and VW. Public in 2020 via SPAC
  • SolidPower: solid state lithium metal. Backed by Ford and BMW
  • The industry is being helped out by the Biden administration
  • For backups to wind and solar: it would be incredibly costly to have large scale lithium ions that back ups an entire city (if they get hit with a storm, for example)
  • Scientists have studied all the metals we could use in batteries, and it seems unlikely that there are materials that will make for vastly better batteries than the ones we were already building. I think we can improve them by a factor of 3, but not by a factor of 50.

Compressed air storage

  • Based off of technology already used in the oil and gas industries
  • Issues
  • Requires a huge site, i.e. a rock cavern, thats stable enough to store high pressure compressed air. This means the technology cant always live where needs are greatest
  • Air needs to be heated prior to compression, and this heating is done by burning natural gas
  • Traditional systems only operate at 40% efficiency. There are some new ones at 60%

Nuclear Fusion

  • Clean, no waste, insane amount of energy. It’s the processes that power the sun.
  • Needs a huge magnetic field to hold the plasma; the plasma will melt the walls if it touches them
  • Challenges:
  • It requires a ton of energy to put into it
  • Best q right now is 0.7. We need a Q of 10-25 to be net
  • ITER in Europe is supposed to get Q=10. Won’t reach full operation until 2035
  • Commonwealth Fusion (out of MIT) is using new materials - high temperature superconductors. CFS is doing some good things too.
  • Still lots of proof of concepts needed - these could lead to additional setbacks
  • Opportunities
  • New materials can come up which lead to more power generated, leading to quicker progress to net energy
  • The concept has more or less been proven on paper
  • In 2025, there should be a device that’s net power positive operating

Supersonic air travel

  • It’s tough to rationalize this being a green technology, but including it here anyways
  • Target markets:
  • Business jet market. Low price elasticity
  • Commercial aviation market
  • Boom Aerospace
  • 88 seats
  • Planning to fly a prototype late 2021 or early 2022
  • Carry passengers by 2029
  • United Airlines made nonrefundable upfront payments ot them. The amounts aren’t public
  • Expected to cost $20 billion in fixed cost
  • Other companies
  • Virgin Galactic and SpaceX have shown interest in this
  • Concorde
  • High ticket prices (as much as $10k) and 2000 crash
  • Boom says they’ll be 75% cheaper operating costs than the Concorde. They say fares will be comparable to subsonic business class
  • Other concerns
  • Sonic boom. Boom says they can make it a soft thump based on better aerodynamics
  • Other companies: Aerion and Spike
  • Aerion (was backed by Boeing) surprisingly folded in early 2021. Not able to raise enough money. This may have been the source behind United’s LOI in 2021 - needed support for Boom and good publicity for United Airlines too.

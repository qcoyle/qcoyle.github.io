Clearly Communicate Complex Information

Tips to be concise

  • Opening sentence < 50 words.
  • Twitter test: reduce each important point down to 140 characters
  • Note: Can also help lead you to solutions

Managing complexity

  • Treat your audience as brilliant smart people who happen to know nothing about this topic
  • Avoid jargon, corporate talk, and vague industry phrases
  • Often use as little technical detail as possible

The message

  • Tell good stories
  • Giving too many numbers can bore listeners
  • Craft the information into a narrative: lay out the conflict, who, and where. How it’s been overcome
  • Note: knowing this info can also give you a roadmap to the solution
  • Use good + concise metaphors
  • This helps “make it click”
  • Draw similarities to something that’s already known
  • “One analogy is worth five explanations”
  • Constantly ask “so what?”
  • You need to give people a reason to care about what you have to say
  • Is your audience 6 or 60? Or both? Frame it in their shoes
  • Ask “what does my audience need to hear?”
  • Ask what your audience knows and how they learn
  • Focus on the problems that are solved instead of the features of the product. Example:
  • Features of the product: I can take two drug names in either the orthographic, or the phonological representation. I can then use a variety of different orthographic and phonological similarity measures, compute a numerical similarity score between those two representations and use that similarity score to predict the probability of confusion. I can then use that probability of confusion, compare it to the actual probability of confusion in the real world and validate my predictive index. 
  • Problem that it solves - much better: About 4 million times per year, people walk into pharmacies in the United States and they walk out with the wrong drug. One of the main reasons that this happens is because drug names are so similar that they're confusing. Out of these 4 million errors, a significant number of people get hurt because they take the wrong drug, or because they fail to get the drug that they need. My work helps fewer people get hurt.
  • Get straight to the solution after the problem. Example:
  • Out of these 4 million errors, a significant number of people get hurt because they take the wrong drug, or because they fail to get the drug that they need. My work helps fewer people get hurt.
  • Give examples, i.e. case studies of successes
  • Summarize the overall message into three points: it can be tough to have more than three takeaways

Structure

  • Start with context: view it as a satellite view down to street level
  • Break down the story into self contained mini talks. If one part goes wrong or gets forgotten, you can just jump to the next one
  • Analogy: a rickety rope bridge where one rope failure collapses it versus a series of stepping stones. If one stone is unstable, just jump to the next one
  • Narrate the journey: checkpoints to tell people where they’re going, where they are, and where they’ve been.
  • Use sound bytes, i.e. a clear memorable line to summarize at each checkpoint

Visual aids

  • Make the information visually appealing:
  • Images
  • Diagrams
  • Custom dashboards
  • Infographics
  • Videos
  • Instagram stories (saved to your highlights)
  • Backwards map during a summary

Behavior

  • Body language: stand straight but reduce tension/stiffness
  • Make eye contact with the audience
  • Don’t try to emulate anyone else. Just be a bigger version of yourself
  • Use adrenaline to your advantage: excitement rather than fear

Practice

  • Places: in front of the mirror, with friends, videos of yourself
  • Memorize key phrases
  • After you think you’ve practiced enough, practice 10 more times

Help from others

  • Ask an inside if the logic of the case makes sense
  • Ask an outsider for help
  • It’s easy to fall into the trap of unconsciously assuming your audience knows as much as you do
  • Helpful people: friends, family
  • Get input from an outsider:
  • Practice the presentation. Get a sense of their engagement
  • Ask them to read your content and explain it back to you. If they struggle, you need to do better

Look out for

  • Watch out for distracting movements, including pacing
  • Drop points - areas of potential ambiguity or skipped steps
  • Leave out unnecessary detail:  this can be hard if you’re enthusiastic about a topic

Clearly communicate complex information - ways to do it

  • In addition to when speaking, good resources to create are:
  • Webinar
  • Tutorials
  • Blog posts
  • Social media. Repeat the same concise message across platforms

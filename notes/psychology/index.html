Bias

Confirmation bias

  • The tendency to interpret and recall information that confirms our preexisting beliefs and values is called confirmation bias.
  • Example: If we hear someone say that one of our favorite colleagues made an inappropriate comment, we may think “there must be a misunderstanding” because it doesn’t fit with our positive view of that person.

Affinity bias

  • The preference for people similar to ourselves or people we know and like. It often causes us to want to work or spend time with others who are more like us, rather than people who are different.

Conformity bias

  • The desire to fit in. We sometimes take cues for appropriate behavior, perspectives, attitudes, and decisions from others, following their lead instead of using our own judgment.
  • Example: For example, a new employee in the department notices that all of the other women in her unit wear make-up every day. Even though she feels more comfortable not wearing it, she also starts wearing make-up to fit in with her colleagues.

Anchoring

  • Where we depend too heavily on the first piece[s] of information we receive during decision-making.

Halo effect

  • tendency for positive impressions of a person to influence how we think and feel about their character.

Microaggressions

  • Demeaning or disrespectful words/behaviors based on someone’s identity. They can happen both intentionally or unintentionally
  • They convey to the recipient that they do not belong or they are not respected
  • Ways to combat them from others:
  • Direct: directly address disrespectful or offensive behavior that you observe, if it is safe to do so. This tactic is best when you know the individuals or are in a position of authority or power in the situation.
  • Distract: interrupt or de-escalate the interaction by changing the subject or refocusing attention elsewhere. This option can be effective when you don’t know the individuals involved or are worried that directly addressing the behavior may make the situation worse for the person being targeted.
  • Delegate: talking to someone else who can help address the situation. This should be someone you trust, and could include the leader of the group or your supervisor. This may be a good option if you feel uncomfortable or unsafe, or if you’re unsure how to address the situation.
  • Delay: depending on the circumstance, it may be better to delay, or take action later. This option works well if the target of the microaggression is speaking for themselves, when you’ve learned about the situation after it happened, or if taking action in the moment may escalate the situation or cause further harm. This could involve checking on the person later to ensure they are OK and offering your support.
  • Steps to repair harm if you commit a microaggression
  • Pick the right time to talk and listen. Make sure you’re ready to offer a sincere apology by reflecting on whether you accept that someone experienced harm, and that you had a role in it. Approach them privately and express that you’d like to apologize. Ask if they’re willing to discuss it with you, and accept their answer if they’re not. If they are, listen to how they feel and avoid becoming defensive; use active listening skills.
  • Apologize: An effective apology is focused on the person who was harmed. Acknowledge what happened and take accountability for your actions. Avoid saying something like, “I’m sorry you were offended”. This avoids taking personal responsibility for the impact of your words or actions. Try something like, “I said something I thought was funny, but I know now that it was disrespectful to you.” While they may choose to accept your apology and forgive you, you should not expect them to do so.
  • Repair and learn: Resolve to be more intentional next time and seek to repair the harm. Identify what you can learn from the experience, and share with them how you’re going to do better if appropriate. One example may be: “Thank you for sharing more of your experience. I understand better now why what I said upset you. I’d like to make this right, if I can. What might be helpful for you?” If you feel too many negative emotions to learn from the experience, like overwhelming guilt or shame, then reach out to a mentor, a professional or confidential employee assistance program, or someone in your community for support to build your own resilience.

Workplace Diversity

Racial battle fatigue

  • Symptoms like depression, anxiety, and high blood pressure that can be caused by experience ongoing race based agressions
  • Studies show that microagressions can lead to this

Responding to others’ experiences

  • One of the best approaches: Actively listen by thanking the person for trusting you with their experience and asking how they would like to be supported.
  • A bad approach: “I know exactly what you mean when you talk about being profiled. I’ve experienced my own types of harassment. We all go through it.” This centers the conversation on you rather than communicating empathy. Equating your experiences with theirs can communicate theirs isn’t significant.

Ally

  • Someone who uses the privilege(s)privilege(s) and power that they may have to support those who do not carry the same privileges or power. It involves using actions and words to support members of another group
  • Active forms of being an ally include being an advocate or accomplice
  • Ways to be a better ally:
  • Learn
  • Reflect on what you’ve learned
  • Notice
  • I.e. notice who is and isn’t in the room for a meeting/project/opportunity, then advocate for greater representation
  • Amplify
  • Speak up to create space for underrepresented people to contribute
  • Speak up

Disclosure obligations

  • In some scenarios (especially for supervisors/managers), you might have an obligation to take further action when a person discloses a concern that could be a violation of the law or our policies
  • Sharing any obligations you have to disclose or report specific information with employees before they begin describing their concern, will allow them to make an informed choice about what they share with you

Ally communication tactics

  1. Active listening: intentionally concentrating on both the words and the message that is being communicated by a speaker. This leads to your better understanding, and the speaker will likely feel respected and validated
  • Especially helpful when communicating with someone who has a different point of view than you
  • Tips:
  • Listen to what’s being said, instead of just to gather info for your own responses
  • Process the main points of the speaker’s message
  • Try repeating back the key points, asking if you interpreted them correctly
  • Let the speaker adjust your interpretation if needed
  • Respond to the speaker
  1. Perspective taking: considering someone else’s point of view, thoughts, and feelings.
  • When:
  • When you’ve joined a group as an ally
  • When there’s disagreements among team members
  • If someone says what you said/did was harmful to them
  • Tips:
  • Avoid trying to convince someone of your viewpoint. Instead, try to understand the situation from their experience
  • Asking permission to reflect back to the person what you imagine they may have experienced to check your understanding. It could sound like: “When I said/did X, I imagine you may have felt Y. Is that right?”
  • Imagine yourself in that person’s place, having a similar experience
  • Think about a time when you may have been in a similar situation or felt the same way
  • Take time to learn about cultures, heritages, and lived experiences that are different from your own
  1. Validating: supporting the value of someone’s perspective and the legitimacy of their feelings when they share an experience with you, even though you may not be able to personally relate. Includes:
  • Thanking them for sharing and trusting you with the information
  • Asking them what they would like for you to do, if anything, with the information
  • Actively listening and asking if they’re open to clarifying questions
  • Centering your responses on their needs
  • Avoid trying to console by diminishing the harm or explaining the intentions of the other person. This response may minimize their experience
  1. Calling out: a powerful ally practice that we should use carefully and respectfully as we look to build a community of allies
  • When to do it
  1. When immediately addressing the words or behavior will prevent further harm
  2. When a leader observing the conduct directly addresses it, to reinforce behavior expectations while role modeling allyship to others
  • When doing it, use constructive phrases like:
  • “That’s not how we interact”
  • “Those aren’t our values”
  • “You may be making an assumption that we need to address/think about”
  • “Have you considered how [your words/actions] impact others?”
  • “I’m not comfortable with how this conversation is going”
  1. Calling in: ally addresses the harmful behavior or words in a way that is often done privately (term coined by scholar-activist Dr. Loretta Ross)
  • When to do it:
  • When the person who acted disrespectfully signals they want to understand the impact of their words and/or actions
  • When the impacted person signals that “calling out” would make the situation worse for them
  • Tips:
  • When doing it, ask a person about their intentions when they said or did something
  • Ask what input/data/criteria a person used to reach their decision or conclusion
  • Ask them to explore how their preferences, prior experiences, expectations, or assumptions could be influencing their actions

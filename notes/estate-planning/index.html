  • These are known as the big four. Without them, the state gives your assets to your closest relatives by default

Terminology

Estate planning

  • Directing what happens to your assets when you die and who should execute these directions
  • It also includes what should happen if you’re incapacitated (type of medical care you do and don’t want), and your desires for funeral arrangments plus how to pay for them
  • Proactively planning can help reduce estate taxes

Will

  • Document that says what happens to your assets after you die
  • This includes:
  • Children/dependents - appoints a guardian
  • Property - what should happen to it?
  • Cars
  • Heirlooms
  • Cash
  • Stocks/bonds
  • Exceptions:
  • Assets (i.e. IRA’s or investment accounts) with a beneficiary already named
  • Where it should be kept: somewhere safe, i.e. a vault in a bank or attorney’s office
  • It should not be locked away where nobody can access it. Breaking open a safe requires a court order, which can take time
  • Note: copies of wills can’t act as wills and copies require court action to be accepted
  • Wills should be updated when large changes occur, for example:
  • Birth of a child, including additional children
  • Buying property
  • Moving to a new state
  • Change in relationship status
  • Gaining/losing a lot of money
  • Wills can help reduce legal fees after death, and they make the process faster

Living will

  • Says what you want to happen if you’re still living but incapacitated
  • Also known as an advance directive or a directive to physicians
  • For example, if you don’t have one doctors may resort to long, expensive, and futile measures even though that’s not what you would have wanted

General power of attorney

  • Giving someone legal power to act on your behalf
  • Most importantly, this is someone who can manage your affairs - i.e. paying your bills, handling financial transactions, paying your taxes, settling claims, and making financial gifts
  • This is a separate legal document from a will
  • Can be specified to not go into effect until a doctor certifies you as incapacitated (see Durable power of attorney)
  • Note: it differs from other powers of attorney in that it covers all cases. Because you might not anticipate every possible legal scenario
  • Note: this can also be used if you are not incapacitated - i.e. you’re vacationing off the grid and need someone to do certain things

Durable power of attorney

  • Dictates that a power of attorney continues to apply if you become incapacitated
  • Unless otherwise specified, it stays effective until you revoke the power you’ve granted, or until you die
  • Sometimes courts will end durable power of attorney - i.e. a divorce occurred but these documents weren’t updated
  • In some states, all power of attorney is treated as durable unless explicitly stated

Durable healthcare power of attorney

  • The person who makes medical decisions for you in the event of incapacitation
  • Can have a broad scope that covers all healthcare decisions, or a narrow scope with respect to specific medical treatments

Durable financial power of attorney

  • Names someone to make legal and financial decisions for you in the event of incapacitation/death

Special power of attorney

  • Is very narrow, requiring every authority that’s granted to be outlined
  • It can refer to a specific financial decision or a specific illness

Funeral plan

  • Part of your will - includes the following:
  • Whether you’d like to be buried, interred in a crypt, or cremated. If you want burial, where will that take place and what type of casket? Crypt → how to store remains
  • Type of funeral service you want to have and where to hold it
  • Budget and how to pay for it. You can prepay funeral expenses, buy life insurance to pay for funeral costs, or have a designated funeral plan savings account

Executor

  • The person you designate as your estate’s legal representative (for your will)
  • The state will appoint one in the event that you haven’t named one
  • This person should know your best interests. They should know (or have instructions for) where things are
  • Note: it can take years to execute a will - selling a house, selling off or converting investments, etc.
  • Executors can pay themselves a fee for their time
  • Typical executors: one child or all children

Estate attorney

  • A lawyer who is board certified in estate planning (most states offer estate planning certifications)
  • The National Association of Estate Planners & Councils is an organization of them
  • Ways to find one
  • Asking people who you trust
  • See if your employer offers one
  • Costs usually between $300 and $1,200

Estate organizer

  • A collection of your documents and accounts so that people can find your assets (or find where to find your assets)
  • Should also contain power of attorney for your digital assets

Power of attorney for your digital assets

  • Should contain access to accounts like: email, cloud storage, passwords
  • Should also limit what people shouldn’t see - i.e. if you don’t want access given to emails or other private accounts
  • If you want a final social media posting (or don’t want one), it should also include a directive on that
  • There are directives that require two (or more) people access info together

Estate taxes

  • The taxes that heirs need to pay on assets above a minimum value, which typically fluctuates among political lines

Medicaid and long-term care

  • It only covers long-term care once people qualify (below a certain worth)
  • Note: getting rid of assets to qualify can often take years

Probate

  • The public court process (states are in control of this) that accepts a will as a valid legal document
  • Often takes a lot of time
  • It’s public
  • Can be avoided through a trust + assets with beneficiary designations. Usually with the help of an estate attorney

Trust

  • All-encompassing instructions for how and when assets are given to beneficiaries
  • It’s much more custom than a will
  • Can help save beneficiaries from going through the probate process
  • Can protect the assets against creditors - losses that the beneficiaries may incur in the future
  • Example directions to include in a trust
  • A beneficiary must graduate college or reach a certain age before getting their assets
  • A beneficiary is given a more-limited and specified amount of assets
  • Where money goes to spouses and children - especially helpful for blended families
  • Direct that funds are used in specific ways, i.e. education, medical, or charitable uses
  • 2 types of trustus: revocable and irrevocable

Revocable trust

  • A trust that you can modify or end during your lifetime
  • Earnings in these trusts are part of income tax returns
  • Assets in a revocable trust can be managed by you or a financial advisor
  • Can lay out trustee(s) in the event you die
  • Also known as a living trust
  • Once you die, the wishes laid out in the trust are final and the trust becomes irrevocable and now managed by your trustee(s)

Irrevocable trust

  • A trust that cannot be modified or ended (both during your life or after) without court order. This often requires help of a lawyer
  • It’s a separate legal entity. It pays its own taxes
  • Common use: creating before you die to move assets out of an estate so that they aren’t subject to estate tax

Questions and answers

Who doesn’t need a will?

  • Young people who don’t have assets, kids, or pets
  • If you are young and don’t have a will, everything will go to your parents

How to make a will?

  • LegalZoom and WillMaker (Quicken) will do it online
  • Help from an attorney, especially if your estate and/or family is large

How to avoid family conflicts?

  • Consider naming all children to executor and health-care roles
  • Split up executor and health care roles equally among children

Consequences of not having a will?

  • State decides what to do - based on statutes
  • Often a source of fallout among heirs: disputes can become costly and long legal battles
  • In some states, spouses aren’t entitled to assets by default (if you’re married)
  • Even if heirs are in agreement about assets, assets can get tied up in probate

Investments that are hard to dispose of?

  • Timeshares are often the most difficult, and the value often becomes minimized or lost

Are charitable donations after you die taxed?

  • No. This includes charitable remainder trusts and donor-advised funds

Examples of trusts?

The below are from Carson Wealth

  • Survivor’s Trusts
  • Created by an individual during their lifetime to provide for a surviving spouse, domestic partner or other loved one(s).
  • Guardianship Trusts
  • Established to protect and handle the assets of minors.
  • Generation-Skipping Trusts
  • Provide distributions for the benefit of a child for life, with the remainder continuing on to grandchildren, or more remote descendants.
  • Aka dynasty trusts
  • Special Needs Trusts
  • Established to ensure any supplemental or living expenses are paid through a disabled person’s lifetime that are not paid by other sources.
  • Charitable Remainder Trusts
  • Irrevocable trusts. Income is paid to designated beneficiaries for a certain term or lifetime. The remainder interest is paid to qualified organizations as specified in the document.
  • Donor-Advised Funds (DAF)
  • Is run by 501(c)(3) organizations that invest the donations and manage the account
  • You can name your DAF as a primary or secondary beneficiary
  • your children can take over some DAFs when you are no longer here or able to manage the fund
  • Is not taxed
  • Private Foundations
  • Charitable organizations created and funded by a donor as a trust or a non-profit organization.
  • Irrevocable Life Insurance Trusts (ILITs)
  • May be used to shelter an insurance death benefit from estate taxes and may provide liquidity to pay estate taxes, settlement costs and/or equalize distributions among beneficiaries.
  • Credit Shelter Trusts
  • Allow married couples, who may have to pay estate taxes, to reduce those taxes by taking full advantage of state and federal estate tax exemptions.

Capital Markets

Definition

  • Place where savings and investments are exchanged between suppliers (people/institutions that have money to invest) and seekers (businesses, governments, or people)
  • Main types of markets
  • Stock market
  • Bond market
  • Currency & foreign exchange markets
  • Two tiers of markets
  • Primary market
  • Secondary marekt
  • They’re an important part of functioning economies. They move money from those who have it to those who need to put it to productive use

Equities

  • Stocks - shares of ownership in a company

Debt securities

  • Interest bearing IOU’s - it’s a document that establishes a debt obligation
  • Main types:
  • Bonds/notes
  • Medium term notes
  • Commercial paper
  • General characteristics:
  • They must be redeemed by the issuer on a specified date or in installments (except in the case of perpetual securities)
  • They can be issued on a stock exchange
  • They can be issued to a pre-selected group of investors
  • They’re usually traded over the counter between two parties (instead of via a stock exchange), even when they’re listed on a stock exahge
  • They can be unsecured (and ranked equally with the issuer’s other unsecured debt) or secured on specific assets

Primary markets

  • Where new equity stock and bond issues are sold to investors
  • All issues on primary markets are subject to strict regulation by the SEC (or other countries’ securities agencies). Filings must be approved before going public
  • Example: IPO’s put shares on primary markets

Secondary markets

  • Previously issued equities are traded among investors
  • These venues for trading are overseen by bodies like the SEC
  • Examples: NYSE and Nasdaq

Bonds vs. stocks

  • Bonds pros
  • Safer investment
  • Receive income through the interest payments
  • You get all your principal back if you hold the bond to maturity
  • Stocks pros
  • Can get a higher return on your investment
  • You can play the long game (i.e. if you’re young) - more time to recoup potential losses
  • Risks to both
  • Companies can default on bonds; stock prices can drop to 0
  • You can resell both - profit or loss if their prices rise/fall

Mutual fund vs. ETF

  • Both:
  • Mix many assets, a way to diversify
  • Assets include stocks, bonds, and short term debt
  • ETF:
  • Can be traded like stocks
  • Passively managed
  • Mutual funds:
  • Actively managed by a manager
  • Only purchased at end of each trading day
  • More of them than ETF’s
  • Usually higher minimum investment requirement, i.e. the Vanguard 500 Index Investor Fund has a 3000 minimum

Debt Securities

Bonds

  • A promise between an investor to borrower in which the borrower will pay back the principal at a specified end date, in addition to terms for variable or fixed interest payments made over time
  • The borrower is usually a corporation or government
  • Can usually be sold by the original lender on secondary markets
  • They can also be repurchased - i.e. if interest rates decline or the borrower’s credit improves

Bond yield

  • The return an investor realizes on a bond
  • Factors in interest payments, time value of money, and compounding interest

Bonds vs. notes payable

  • Similarities
  • Financially they’re pretty much indistinguishable. Both show up on the liabilities of a company’s balance sheet; interest paid is an expense on income statement
  • Differences
  • Securities laws: bonds are always regulated as securities, notes payable are not
  • The term of the debt: debts with a maturity of less than a year are usually notes; longer term ones are typically bonds. For instance, a U.S treasury note has a maturity of 1-10 years. A U.S. treasury bond has a maturity of >10 years (note: treasury debt with maturities less than a year are treasury bills)
  • Note: unless you’re a lawyer, professional debt trader, or securities regulator, you don’t usually need to worry about these differences

Variable interest rate

  • An interest rate for a loan that fluctuates over time based on other benchmarks that can change
  • It’s typically linked to the LIBOR or federal funds rate
  • Common example uses: mortgages, credit cards, corporate bonds, or derivatives
  • Aka adjustable or floating rate\

Fixed interest rate

  • An unchanging interest rate placed on a debt
  • Attractive to borrowers who don’t want interest rates fluctuating over the term of their loans
  • Note: there are also hybrid interest rates between variable and fixed

Variable vs. fixed interest rates

  • Variable pros
  • Can be lower than fixed rates
  • If rates decline, you pay less
  • It can be time-consuming and expensive to refinance a fixed interest rate to a lower one
  • Fixed pros
  • Predictability
  • Can be better to do when interest rates are low
  • Can be easier to calculate long term costs to borrow

Unsecured debt

  • Loans not backed by collateral
  • It’s considered riskier than secured debt for this reason. Carries higher interest rates as such
  • Corporate bonds can be secured or unsecured. Federal bonds are unsecured
  • Examples:
  • Credit cards
  • Medical bills
  • Utility bills

Secured debt

  • Debt backed by collateral so that there is less risk for the lender
  • Usually carries lower interest rates than unsecured debt

Recourse debt

  • A type of secured debt that lets lenders recoup defaulted loan balances by seizing the borrower’s assets in addition to loan collateral

Full recourse vs. limited recourse

  • Ways to describe debt
  • Limited recourse: the creditor can claim certain but not all assets of the borrower if the borrower defaults
  • Full recourse: creditors can claim any assets of the borrower to fully cover the unpaid loan

Perpetual securities

  • Debt securities issues without a fixed redemption date

Pari passu

  • Latin for “equal footing” - in finance it means two or more parties to a contract/claim are treated the same
  • Commonly used for dividing assets in bankruptcy proceedings or wills/trusts - i.e. everyone getting a fractional amount at the same time

Equity Securities

Options

  • The right for you to buy shares of stock in the company at a predetermined price in the future. They’re the way you purchase shares
  • They are not cash and should not be relied upon - something to consider when getting compensation

Issue date

  • The date that a stock option is granted to you

Shares

  • Your ownership of the company in a fraction of the company’s overall stock after you buy vested options
  • They don’t have a time limit and many people still hang onto them after leaving a company

Strike price

  • Purchase price for which you can exercise each option - how you convert options into shares
  • The strike price offered in options is locked in and shouldn’t change, even as the company’s value of shares (and total share quantity) increases

Vesting

  • Pre-agreed to timeframe and rules for when you can access stock options that are offered
  • You can’t exercise some options until a certain time period has elapsed
  • Many companies offer a standard 4-year vesting period with one year cliff
  • One way to do it is you get 25% of options after the cliff, and the remainder vest proportionally month to month

Exercise

  • When you buy your vested options at the pre-determined strike price, you are vesting them. Upon this purchase, you get shares of the company
  • Note: you usually pay to exercise via cash or deferred payment

Stock options plan

  • A legal document that details your stock option rights
  • You may want to consult legal advice/outside opinion on this document
  • Once you sign it, it’s pretty binding and not much you can do to change it

Success event

  • A sale or IPO

Triggers

  • Sometimes options are accelerated for you to purchase them before a 4-year vesting period if a company gets acquired. This is usually stated in the stock options plan. Some agreements leave this decision up to the company’s board of directors
  • Single vs. double trigger:
  • Single: Full or partial acceleration of unvested options after a success event
  • Double: Less common and usually reserved for cofounders or executives. Typically requires the sale of the company and the second is the termination of the employee without cause - this protects founders/execs that can be terminated after an acquisition

RSU

  • Restricted stock units: the grant is “restricted” by being subject to a vesting schedule
  • A way an employer can grant company shares. Unlike options they’re typically always worth something, even if stock price drops below what it was on the grant date
  • They must vest before they can be received. Quitting/getting fired usually stops vesting
  • Once the shares are received after the vesting date, the receiver gets voting and dividend rights
  • Sometimes companies pay dividend equivalent payouts for unvested RSU’s (these get reported on your W2 as wages, so they’re at a higher rate)
  • Taxation: you get taxed when the shares are delivered at vesting. They’re taxed as income of the market value of those shares at vesting
  • Often gets withheld by the employer
  • Typically, companies take this tax off by surrendering some of the stock back to the company and then company cash is used for that payroll tax deposit
  • When you later sell the shares, they get taxed again as capital gains

Roadshow

  • Company’s leadership and investment bankers travel to meet with potential investors to convince them to buy shares - typically in an IPO
  • When companies go public, they (and their investment bankers) want to sell all their available securities in a quick period of time on a Primary market
  • As such, they must market to large investors who can buy a lot of securities at once. This is often done in a roadshow
  • Note: small investors are usually unable to buy shares on a primary market

Private Capital

Alternative investment

  • A financial asset that doesn’t fall into the conventional investment categories of stocks, bonds, and cash
  • Includes private equity, hedge funds, managed futures, art, commodities, real estate, and derivatives.

Private equity

  • Funds and investors that invest directly in private companies or buy out public companies
  • Private equity funds make money by charging fees to the investors in the fund
  • Typically 2% annually on all assets and 20% of profits from sales of companies
  • Investors in a fund are typically institutions or accredited investors
  • Includes leveraged buyouts and venture capital

Accredited investor

  • Person or business that is allowed to trade securities which aren’t registered with financial authorities
  • You get this privileged access by having certain income, net worth, asset size, or experience (financial sophistication with reduced need for protection provided by regulations)
  • Examples: High net worth people, banks, insurance companies, brokers, and trusts

Private equity company structure

  • Typically has limited partners (LP) owning a vast majority of the shares and general partners (GP) who own a small percent of shares and have full liability
  • The fund then owns a portfolio of investments
  • Typical limited partners (investors)
  • Public pension funds
  • Corporate pension funds
  • Insurance companies
  • Rich people and family offices
  • Endowments
  • Foundations
  • Sovereign wealth funds

Holding period

  • Private equity companies typically require long holding periods for investors - in order to adequately allow for liquidity events like IPO’s or sales

Limited partner

  • A partial owner (investor) of a company who invests money in the company in exchange for shares
  • They have restricted voting power and no day to day involvement in the business
  • Their liability does not exceed how much that person invested in the company
  • In some states, a limited partner who participates in a partnership for >500 hours a year will be viewed as a general partner
  • For taxes, limited partner income is considered passive income by the IRS. Limited partners don’t pay self employment taxes like general partners do

General partner

  • One of 2 or more investors who jointly own a business structured as a partnership, but this partner has the day to day role in managing it
  • They can act on the half of the business without permission from limited partners
  • Can be held personally liable for company debts - can have unlimited liability (unlike a limited partner)
  • Examples: A doctor, lawyer, creative, or investor

Partnership

  • Any business formed by at least two people agreeing to share a company’s expenses and profits
  • Still allows for scaling by adding limited partners
  • Allows people to be their own bosses

Limited partnership (LP)

  • A company with at least one general partner and one limited partner
  • Main benefit: it isn’t taxed separately, so no corporate taxes on profits. Each partner gets their share of profits as income and pays taxes on that
  • The downside is the general partner being personally responsible for liabilities of the partnership - i.e. suing a doctor for medical malpractice. Their personal assets could get liquidated

Pros and cons of private equity

  • Pros
  • Access to liquidity as an alternative to high interest bank loans or being on public markets
  • Can facilitate risky new ventures (venture capital)
  • Can facilitate risky, unconventional growth strategies for delisted companies by keeping things away from public markets and the pressure of quarterly earnings
  • Cons
  • Can be hard to liquidate holdings in private equity - there’s no market that matches buyers with sellers. Firms need to search for buyers themselves
  • Pricing of shares is determined through negotiations between buyers and sellers, not through market forces
  • Rights of private equity shareholders are negotiated case-by-case - not general governance like you get in public markets

History of private equity

  • First leveraged buyout to be when J.P. Morgan did the first leveraged buyout of Carnegie Steel in 1901 to make US Steel
  • Consolidations like this done by banks were ended with the 1933 Glass Steagall Act
  • 1988: KKR buying RJR Nabisco was the biggest private equity deal in history at the time. $25.1 B
  • 2007: GS, TPG Capital, and KKR raised debt to buy TXU Energy (coal plant operator) for $45 B. This is the biggest private equity deal in history. The venture went bankrupt in 2014
  • 2005-2007 were a huge boom in private equity. $2 T was raised. A Harvard study found companies backed by PE performed better than counterparts

Types of private equity funds

  • Distressed funding
  • Leveraged buyouts
  • Real estate private equity
  • Fund of funds
  • Venture capital

Distressed funding

  • Funding that invests in underperforming/troubled companies
  • Tries to turn them around by making changes to management, operations, or sale of assets
  • Commonly done with companies that file for Chapter 11 bankruptcy
  • Aka vulture financing

Leveraged buyouts

  • Buying out a company completely to improve its business and then resell it for a profit - either to another party or via an IPO
  • Is also commonly done by selling non-core business units of public companies (spin off)
  • How it works:
  • PE firm creates a special purpose vehicle to fund the takeover
  • A combination of debt and equity is used to finance the deal - most of it often debt
  • PE firm tries to turn company around, i.e. by
  • Layoffs
  • Replacing management
  • New strategy
  • The most popular type of private equity funding

Special purpose vehicle

  • A subsidiary, with separate legal status as a company, created by a company to keep financial risk separate. It can also hold debt to be used for a leveraged buyout
  • Has its own balance sheet
  • Isolates a risky project from the parent company
  • Note: these have been used as a way to hide debt, i.e. with Enron. As such, as an investor you should always check the financials of SPV’s (and not just the parent company) when investing in said parent company
  • Also called a special purpose entity (SPE)

Real estate private equity

  • Professionally managed investments in real estate markets
  • The investment fund acquires, finances, and owns properties
  • It’s usually riskier and requires more upfront capital than other types of real estate investment, but has higher returns

Fund of funds

  • A private equity fund that invests in other funds - usually mutual funds and hedge funds
  • They allow investors who don’t have minimal capital requirements to invest in certain mutual/hedge funds to still invest in them
  • They have higher management fees

Venture capital

  • A subset of private equity that provides startups and small business that are believed to have long term growth potential
  • Typical sources:
  • Wealthy investors
  • Investment banks
  • Other financial institutions
  • In addition to money, it can also be provided in the form of technical/managerial expertise
  • Common for new companies that have a limited operating history and are lacking access to capital markets, bank loans, or other ways to raise debt

Benefits and risks of venture capital

  • Benefits:
  • A way to get money when you otherwise couldn’t
  • Possible risks (or rewards):
  • Investors get equity in the company
  • Investors get a say in company decisions

Private equity vs. venture capital

  • Venture capital is for much smaller companies - startups and small businesses that can take advantage of new innovation or capitalize on a new industry niche
  • Venture capital usually has 50% or less ownership in their companies - much more diversified
  • Venture capital is usually limited to startups in technology (including biotech and cleantech)
  • Venture capital provides equity only; PE uses both cash and debt when investing
  • Private equity usually buys mature established companies to flip them. As such, there is less of a chance of absolute losses (unlike an investment in a startup)
  • Private equity often buys 100% ownership in companies they invest - less diversified

Venture capital LPs vs GPs

  • General partners: the people who decide what to invest in and take board seats
  • Limited partners: the people whose money gets invested. They are usually happy as long as the fund is doing well

Private credit funds

  • Private equity that raise money from institutional investors (i.e. pension funds) to give a line of credit for companies that can’t raise debt via bonds
  • They are also less affected by geopolitical issues like the bond market is
  • They have shorter time periods (vs. normal PE funds) and charge high interest rates
  • They are less regulated than most of the financial industry
  • They have become an increasing share of business at private equity funds since the financial crisis

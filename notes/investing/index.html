Markets

Mutual fund vs. ETF

  • Both:
  • Mix many assets, a way to diversify
  • ETF:
  • Can be traded like stocks
  • Passively managed
  • Mutual funds:
  • Actively managed by a manager
  • Only purchased at end of each trading day
  • More of them than ETF’s
  • Usually higher minimum investment requirement, i.e. the Vanguard 500 Index Investor Fund has a 3000 minimum

Employers

Options

  • The right for you to buy shares of stock in the company at a predetermined price in the future. They’re the way you purchase shares
  • They are not cash and should not be relied upon - something to consider when getting compensation

Issue date

  • The date that a stock option is granted to you

Shares

  • Your ownership of the company in a fraction of the company’s overall stock after you buy vested options
  • They don’t have a time limit and many people still hang onto them after leaving a company

Strike price

  • Purchase price for which you can exercise each option - how you convert options into shares
  • The strike price offered in options is locked in and shouldn’t change, even as the company’s value of shares (and total share quantity) increases

Vesting

  • Pre-agreed to timeframe and rules for when you can access stock options that are offered
  • You can’t exercise some options until a certain time period has elapsed
  • Many companies offer a standard 4-year vesting period with one year cliff
  • One way to do it is you get 25% of options after the cliff, and the remainder vest proportionally month to month

Exercise

  • When you buy your vested options at the pre-determined strike price, you are vesting them. Upon this purchase, you get shares of the company
  • Note: you usually pay to exercise via cash or deferred payment

Stock options plan

  • A legal document that details your stock option rights
  • You may want to consult legal advice/outside opinion on this document
  • Once you sign it, it’s pretty binding and not much you can do to change it

Success event

  • A sale or IPO

Triggers

  • Sometimes options are accelerated for you to purchase them before a 4-year vesting period if a company gets acquired. This is usually stated in the stock options plan. Some agreements leave this decision up to the company’s board of directors
  • Single vs. double trigger:
  • Single: Full or partial acceleration of unvested options after a success event
  • Double: Less common and usually reserved for cofounders or executives. Typically requires the sale of the company and the second is the termination of the employee without cause - this protects founders/execs that can be terminated after an acquisition

RSU

  • Restricted stock units: the grant is “restricted” by being subject to a vesting schedule
  • A way an employer can grant company shares. Unlike options they’re typically always worth something, even if stock price drops below what it was on the grant date
  • They must vest before they can be received. Quitting/getting fired usually stops vesting
  • Once the shares are received after the vesting date, the receiver gets voting and dividend rights
  • Sometimes companies pay dividend equivalent payouts for unvested RSU’s (these get reported on your W2 as wages, so they’re at a higher rate)
  • Taxation: you get taxed when the shares are delivered at vesting. They’re taxed as income of the market value of those shares at vesting
  • Often gets withheld by the employer
  • Typically, companies take this tax off by surrendering some of the stock back to the company and then company cash is used for that payroll tax deposit
  • When you later sell the shares, they get taxed again as capital gains
